Prompting: Five Techniques

📄 Video script / lecture notes 📄 Chain-of-thought examples

The five prompting techniques at a glance:

Zero-
shot
Zero-shot Prompting
Giving simple, direct instructions to an AI tool without providing any examples. Relies on the model's pre-trained knowledge.
e.g. "Summarize this paragraph in two sentences."
Few-
shot
Few-shot Prompting
Giving an AI tool examples of what you want it to generate before making your request. Helps guide the output format and style.
e.g. Showing the AI two example email greetings, then asking it to write a third.
Chain
of thought
Chain-of-thought Prompting
Asking an AI tool to explain things step-by-step, showing its reasoning. Breaks down complex problems into individual steps.
e.g. "Explain your reasoning step by step before giving your answer."
Role
prompting
Role Prompting
Asking an AI tool to take on a specific perspective or persona when responding to your requests. Tailors the output to a specific audience or purpose.
e.g. "You are an experienced academic writing tutor. Review this introduction and give feedback."
Iterative
Iterative Prompting
A process where you refine your requests to an AI tool based on the AI's responses. You improve the prompt progressively until you get the output you need.
e.g. Starting with a broad request, then refining: "Make it more formal" → "Shorten it" → "Add a call to action."

📄 The Writing Process ▶

Generated by prompting diffit.me

The writing process is a journey that transforms thoughts into polished pieces of writing. It is not a rigid set of rules, but rather a dynamic and evolving process that guides writers from initial inspiration to the final product. This process can be broken down into key steps: prewriting, drafting, revising, and editing and proofreading.

Prewriting is the foundation, where writers generate ideas and plan the structure of their work. This stage involves brainstorming, outlining, and organizing thoughts. It helps writers clarify their purpose, audience, and main ideas — like laying the groundwork for a building.

Drafting is where writers put pen to paper and compose the first draft. The focus is on getting ideas down without worrying too much about perfection — capturing the essence of the idea, not creating a masterpiece.

Revising is where writers step back and review their draft, making structural changes, refining the organization, clarifying ideas, and improving the overall flow. It is like adding details and refining the composition of a painting.

Editing and proofreading is a detailed review focused on correcting grammar, punctuation, spelling, and other mechanical aspects of writing. Proofreading is the final inspection before presenting the finished work.

📄 AI in the Writing Process ▶

Generated by prompting diffit.me

The rise of artificial intelligence has sparked both excitement and apprehension, particularly in the realm of writing. While some fear that AI tools will replace human writers, others see them as valuable assistants that can enhance the writing process. AI writing tools can be used throughout the writing process — from prewriting to proofreading — offering a range of features that can improve quality and efficiency.

One of the most significant benefits of AI writing tools is their ability to generate ideas and suggestions. These tools analyze vast amounts of data to identify popular and trending topics, which can be particularly helpful for writers struggling to come up with a topic or who want to ensure relevance to their target audience.

AI writing tools can also assist with drafting by providing suggestions for additional ideas and writing style, and can improve flow and readability by suggesting alternative word choices and sentence structures.

Once a draft is complete, AI writing tools can be used to revise and edit the text — helping writers improve clarity, conciseness, and flow. They can even generate citations and bibliographies. For proofreading, AI tools help identify and correct errors in grammar, punctuation, spelling, and style, and ensure consistency in tone and voice.

However, AI tools cannot replace human creativity, judgment, and the ability to connect with an audience. They are assistants, not authors.

📄 Prompting Techniques ▶

Generated by prompting diffit.me

Prompting is a crucial skill when working with large language models (LLMs). Prompt engineering is the art of crafting precise instructions to guide LLMs towards specific tasks and desired outputs.

Zero-shot prompting provides a direct instruction or question without any examples, relying solely on the LLM's pre-trained knowledge. It is useful for simple tasks or general knowledge queries.

Few-shot prompting provides a small number of examples before asking the model to perform a task. This technique helps guide the model's output format and style, significantly improving performance on specific tasks.

Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting breaks down complex problems into individual steps and feeds them to the model one at a time. By breaking down the task into smaller, logical steps, the model's reasoning capabilities are enhanced.

Role prompting assigns a specific role to the LLM, which can influence its response style and content — for example, asking it to act as a customer service representative, a technical writer, or a creative storyteller.

Iterative prompting is a dynamic process of continuous refinement, where the user evaluates each response, identifies areas for improvement, and crafts a new prompt that builds on the model's output. It is particularly valuable for complex or creative tasks.

🐒 The Infinite Monkey Theorem and Generative AI ▶

Generated by prompting diffit.me

The Infinite Monkey Theorem is a mind-bending idea: if you had an infinite number of monkeys banging away on typewriters for an infinite amount of time, they would eventually, by sheer chance, produce the complete works of William Shakespeare. It is a thought experiment highlighting the power of probability and randomness.

In reality, a recent study showed that it would take far longer than the lifespan of our universe for a monkey to randomly produce Shakespeare. Mathematicians at the University of Technology Sydney (UTS) crunched the numbers — even a hypothetical army of 200,000 chimpanzees typing at lightning speed until the end of time would not produce Hamlet anytime soon.

Enter generative AI. Generative AI systems are like the monkeys with typewriters, but instead of random keystrokes, they use complex algorithms to generate content based on the data they have been trained on. Generative AI is not randomly producing words — it is simulating how language works, predicting the most likely next word based on context and training data.

This is both generative AI's strength and limitation: it produces statistically likely, coherent-sounding text, but not necessarily true, original, or creative text in the human sense.
